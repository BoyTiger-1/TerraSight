About TerraSight

Built because the smoke reached our street.

The story

Two years ago a wildfire broke out an hour from my community. For days the air was thick with smoke, evacuation alerts loomed, and my family packed bags we hoped we would not need. We were lucky. The LA fires that followed showed how many people are not.

I started with a single wildfire prediction model. It worked, but the more I learned, the clearer it became that no hazard exists alone: the drought feeds the fire, the fire poisons the air, the burn scar sheds the debris flow. So the one model became fifteen coordinated modules, and the demo data became real satellite feeds, reanalysis archives, river gauges, and seismic catalogs.

TerraSight is what I wished existed when we were watching that smoke column: one place that says what is likely, why, how confident it is, and what to do.

Design principles
Real data or no data.
Every number traces to NASA, NOAA, USGS, or Open-Meteo. The training sets are documented historical disasters matched with ERA5 reanalysis, and each module lists its sources.
Explainable, or it does not ship.
Each prediction shows the variables that drove it, a confidence label, and a model card with cross-validated performance.
Honest about the limits of science.
Earthquakes and eruptions cannot be predicted. Those modules model impact and readiness instead, and say so on the page.
Physics beats overfitting.
The ML models are monotonically constrained: more wind can never mean less fire, more rain can never mean more drought.
